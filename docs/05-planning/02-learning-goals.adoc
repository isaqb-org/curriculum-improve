[[LZ-5-01]]
==== LZ 5-01: Darstellen von bewerteten Problemen und Lösungsansätzen mit geeigneten Werkzeugen

Softwarearchitekt:innen kennen technische und manuelle Ansätze, um bewertete Probleme und Lösungsansätze explizit darzustellen, und können für die jeweilige Situation geeignete Möglichkeiten auswählen.
Sie bevorzugen die kleinsten oder einfachsten Ansätze, um Annahmen explizit zu machen, Abhängigkeiten und Risiken hervorzuheben und all diese aktuell zu halten.


[[LZ-5-02]]
==== LZ 5-02: Leitprinzipien für Verbesserungen anwenden

Softwarearchitekt:innen sollten ihre Planung auf einer Reihe von Leitprinzipien aufbauen. Diese Prinzipien beschreiben eine Denkweise (_mindset_), die die gesamte Verbesserungsinitiative leiten sollte.
Diese Denkweise wird während der Planungsphase etabliert und stellt sicher, dass die resultierende Roadmap (siehe <<LZ-5-03>>) von Anfang an iterativ, feedback-gesteuert und risikobewusst angelegt wird.


[[LZ-5-03]]
==== LZ 5-03: Verbesserungs-Roadmaps entwickeln

Basierend auf den bewerteten Problemen und ausgewählten Lösungsansätzen können Softwarearchitekt:innen mittel- bis langfristige Verbesserungs-Roadmaps erstellen.

Dies beinhaltet:

* Priorisierung von Verbesserungsmaßnahmen (z.B. basierend auf Risiko, Kosten-Nutzen oder Abhängigkeiten).
* Gruppierung von Maßnahmen in logische, inkrementelle Schritte oder Releases.
* Definition von kurz- und langfristigen Zielen.
* Visualisierung des Plans (z.B. als Zeitachse oder als Abfolge von Arbeitspaketen).
* Regelmäßige Kommunikation vornehmen, ausgerichtet auf Stakeholder der Entscheidungsebene.

[[LG-5-01]]
==== LG 5-01: Represent evaluated problems and solution approaches using appropriate tools

Software architects know technical and manual approaches to explicitly represent evaluated problems and solution approaches and choose suitable approaches for the given situation.
They prefer the smallest or simplest approaches to make assumptions explicit, highlight dependencies and risks, and keep all of these up to date.

Examples:

* Issue trackers
* Tables or lightweight databases
* Presentations
* Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)
* Diagrams
* Dashboards (live KPIs and progress tied to planned measures)

[[LG-5-02]]
==== LG 5-02: Apply Guiding Principles for Improvement

Software architects should base their planning on a set of guiding principles. 
These principles are about defining a mindset that should govern the entire improvement initiative.
This mindset is established during the planning phase and ensures the resulting roadmap (see <<LG-5-03>>) is iterative, feedback-driven, and risk-aware from the outset.

Key principles include:

* **Strive for rapid and frequent feedback:** Create tight feedback loops with stakeholders, users, and the system itself (e.g., via testing and monitoring) to continuously validate the direction of the improvement.
* **Implement improvements iteratively:** Avoid "big bang" changes where possible. Deliver value in small, manageable increments to reduce risk and demonstrate progress.
* **Reduce complexity:** Actively seek opportunities to simplify the system, its architecture, and its codebase as part of the improvement work. (see also <<LG-4-04>> and <<LG-4-05>>)
* **Validate improvements with prototypes:** Use proofs-of-concept, spikes, or prototypes to test assumptions and reduce technical or business risk before committing to a full implementation. (see also <<LG-4-03>>)
* **Test after every change:** Maintain a high level of quality and prevent regressions by integrating automated testing into the daily workflow.
* **Communicate assumptions explicitly:** Make all underlying assumptions visible to stakeholders to ensure alignment and avoid misunderstandings later in the process.
* **Group improvement approaches or tactics:** Bundle related improvement tasks into logical work packages or themes.
This reduces setup costs (e.g., context switching, tool setup, team coordination) by allowing similar activities to be performed together, leading to more efficient execution and a more coherent plan. (see also <<LG-5-03>>)


[[LG-5-03]]
==== LG 5-03: Develop Improvement Roadmaps

Based on the evaluated problems and selected approaches, software architects can create mid- to long-term improvement roadmaps.

This includes:

* Prioritizing improvement measures (e.g., based on risk, cost-benefit, and dependencies).
* Grouping measures into logical, incremental steps or releases.
* Defining short-term and long-term goals.
* Visualizing the plan (e.g., as a timeline or a sequence of work packages).

[[LG-5-04]]
==== LG 5-04: [OPTIONAL] Apply Methods for Prioritizing and Planning Improvements

Software architects should be aware of established methods to support planning and prioritization.
The following list is for reference; in-depth knowledge is not required by this curriculum.

Prioritization Methods:

* Value vs. Effort Matrix
* Cost of Delay
* Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF)

Planning and Visualization Methods:

* User Story Mapping
* Kanban boards
* Wardley Maps (see also <<hiredThought>> and <<tune>>)
* Core Domain Charts (see also <<dddCrew>> and <<tune>>)
* North Star (see also <<geeks>> and <<tune>>)


[[LG-5-05]]
==== LG 5-05: [OPTIONAL] Align Improvement Plans with Strategy and Engage Stakeholders

Software architects can effectively align improvement plans with strategic objectives and manage stakeholder engagement.


This includes:

* Understanding business objectives and aligning improvement initiatives with them.
* Watching for upcoming challenges (e.g., changes, growth, extreme situations, trends, market developments) and incorporating them into the improvement strategy.
* Preparing decision-makers for long-term improvement processes by involving them, informing them about goals, benefits, risks, obstacles, and consequences, and obtaining their commitment.
* Building understanding and a network of supporters for improvements through proactive stakeholder management and collaborative architecture work.
* Communicating and arguing approaches to long-term improvement to different stakeholders.

[INFO]
====
**Note:** In many organizations, strategic alignment and stakeholder management are primarily the responsibilities of management or enterprise architects.
Marking this learning goal as optional shall encourage architects to develop these skills where context and authority permit, without making them a required competency in all roles or organizations.
